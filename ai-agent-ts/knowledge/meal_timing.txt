Meal Timing and Frequency - Evidence-Based Guidelines

Meal timing can affect energy levels, performance, and body composition, but total daily intake matters most.

General principles:
- Eat when it fits your schedule and hunger cues; no single "best" meal frequency
- Most people do well with 3-4 meals; some prefer smaller, more frequent meals
- Consistency matters more than exact timing for weight management

Pre-workout (1-2 hours before):
- Carbs for energy; moderate protein; low fat/fiber to avoid digestive discomfort
- Example: banana with peanut butter, oatmeal, or toast

Post-workout (within 2 hours):
- Protein (20-40g) for muscle repair; carbs to replenish glycogen
- Example: chicken with rice, Greek yogurt with fruit, protein shake

Breakfast:
- Not mandatory for weight loss; eat if hungry
- Protein-rich breakfast can improve satiety and reduce snacking

Intermittent fasting:
- 16:8 or similar can work for some; not superior to standard eating for fat loss
- May help with appetite control; avoid if you have a history of disordered eating

Night eating:
- Eating late does not inherently cause weight gain; total calories matter
- Some find that eating close to bedtime affects sleep quality
